Phase 1
Week 1 – 2

The Pause
Know Yourself Before You Build Anything

"You can't design a life you haven't examined."

Most people skip this phase entirely. They jump to goals and plans without understanding who they are, what they actually value, or where their life currently stands. This phase slows you down — intentionally — so everything you build later is anchored in truth, not wishful thinking.

✏️ Exercise

The Life Inventory — Wheel of Life

A complete audit across 8 domains: Faith, Family, Health, Vocation, Finances, Relationships, Growth, and Rest.

Why This Matters

You cannot improve what you haven't honestly assessed. This exercise creates a baseline — your current reality map — so your goals are corrective, not random.

Steps

  1. 1Draw a circle divided into 8 equal sections. Label each: Faith, Family, Health, Vocation, Finances, Relationships, Growth, Rest.
  2. 2Score each area honestly 1–10 based on how satisfied you feel RIGHT NOW (not how important it is).
  3. 3Shade each segment inward from the center to your score.
  4. 4Notice which areas feel deflated. These are your "design gaps."
  5. 5Ask: "Which 2 areas, if improved, would most elevate everything else?"

Reflection Prompts

"Where am I pretending things are fine when they're not?"
"Which area do I consistently neglect — and what story do I tell myself about why?"
🪞 Reflection

The Deathbed Test — Reverse Regret

Project yourself to the end of your life and look backward. What do you wish you had done more of? Less of? Started sooner?

Why This Matters

Urgency only becomes clear when you see the finite nature of your time. This isn't morbid — it's clarifying. The biggest regrets in life are almost never about risks taken; they're about things left undone.

The Exercise

  1. 1Find 20 minutes of complete silence. No phone.
  2. 2Imagine you are 85 years old, looking back at your life.
  3. 3Write freely for 10 minutes answering: "What did I spend my life on? Was it worth it?"
  4. 4Then write: "What do I wish I had prioritized, pursued, or protected?"
  5. 5Circle the 3 most painful answers. These become design constraints — things you will not sacrifice.

Reflection Prompts

"Am I living a life I actually chose — or one I drifted into?"
"What would the 85-year-old version of me beg the present-day me to start doing today?"
🔧 Tool

The Energy Audit — Energy Map

Track what activities drain or replenish you over one week. Design your life around your energy, not just your time.

Why This Matters

Busyness is not the same as productivity. Most people are exhausted not because they do too much — but because they spend too much time doing things that deplete them.

How to Use It

  1. 1For 7 days, after each major activity, rate your energy: ↑ Energized / → Neutral / ↓ Drained.
  2. 2At week's end, list your top 5 "energy drains" and top 5 "energy sources."
  3. 3Ask: "What on my drain list is non-negotiable? What can I eliminate, delegate, or reduce?"
  4. 4Ask: "Am I scheduling enough of what energizes me?"

Key Insight

🔑 Your energy profile is unique. What energizes your colleague or spouse may drain you. Stop designing your life around what should energize you — and start with what actually does.
✏️ Exercise

The Identity Statement — Who Am I?

Write a clear, honest description of who you are right now — not who you hope to be, but who you actually are today.

The Exercise

  1. 1Complete these sentences without overthinking: "I am someone who…" (list 10 honest statements)
  2. 2Then complete: "I want to become someone who…" (list 10)
  3. 3Circle the gaps between who you are and who you want to become. These gaps become your growth targets.

Reflection Prompts

"If I'm being honest, what kind of person do my daily choices reflect — regardless of what I say I believe?"
"What beliefs about myself are holding my life in place — and are they actually true?"
Phase 2
Week 3 – 4

The Core
Define What You Actually Stand For

"A life without a defined center drifts to whoever is loudest."

Your values are not what you say you believe — they are what your calendar and your bank account reveal you believe. This phase forces honest reckoning with your true priorities, so that your future design is built on solid ground rather than borrowed ideals.

✏️ Exercise

Values Excavation — The Top 5

Identify the 5 core values that must be honored for you to feel that your life is meaningful, not just successful.

Steps

  1. 1Write down 20–30 values that resonate (e.g. faith, integrity, freedom, family, growth, service, creativity, security…)
  2. 2Narrow to 10 by asking: "Which ones would I refuse to compromise, even for great reward?"
  3. 3Narrow to 5 by comparing pairs: "If I had to choose between X and Y, which would I keep?"
  4. 4For each of your 5, write: what it means to you personally, and how you are currently honoring (or violating) it.

Reflection Prompts

"Where in my life am I saying one thing and doing another? What does that tell me about my real values?"
"Which of my 5 values do I most frequently sacrifice under pressure?"
🪞 Reflection

Faith Integration — Your Anchor

Explore how your faith forms the foundation of your life design — not as an add-on, but as the root system everything else grows from.

The Exercise

  1. 1Write your answer to: "What does my faith say about why I exist and what I'm here to do?"
  2. 2Ask: "Where am I designing my life as if my faith doesn't matter — and where is it deeply embedded?"
  3. 3List 3 ways you want your faith to be more visibly expressed in your daily decisions, relationships, and work.

Reflection Prompts

"Is my faith a compartment of my life, or is it the lens through which I see all of life?"
"What would it look like to fully surrender my plans to something larger than my own ambitions?"
🔧 Tool

Family Roles Map — What They Need

Clarify the specific roles you play in your family, what each person needs from you, and where you are most absent.

How to Use It

  1. 1List every person in your immediate family.
  2. 2For each, write: the role you play for them (e.g. provider, protector, listener, playmate, spiritual guide).
  3. 3Rate honestly (1–10): how well are you currently fulfilling each role?
  4. 4Ask each family member (if appropriate): "What's one thing you wish I did more of with you?"
  5. 5Circle your biggest gap. Build one specific habit in Phase 5 to close it.

Key Insight

🔑 Family sacrifice is not the cost of ambition — it is an ambition failure. Your family is not competing with your purpose; they are part of it.
🌱 Practice

The Non-Negotiables List

Create a protected list of the things that are NOT for sale — commitments you will not trade for opportunity, productivity, or pressure.

Steps

  1. 1List everything in your life that, if you lost it, would hollow out everything else (e.g. morning prayer, Sunday family dinner, one-on-one time with each child).
  2. 2For each item, write why it is non-negotiable in one sentence.
  3. 3Create a rule: any schedule, opportunity, or request that violates a non-negotiable requires a very strong override justification.

Reflection Prompts

"What have I said is important but consistently cancelled when something 'urgent' comes up?"
Phase 3
Week 5 – 6

The Vision
Build the Picture of Your Preferred Future

"Where there is no vision, the people wander. Where there is clarity, they move."

Vision is not fantasy. It's a detailed, emotionally compelling picture of what your life looks like when it's working — aligned with your values, your faith, and your family. This phase builds that picture so concretely that you can feel it before you achieve it.

✏️ Exercise

The 10-Year Letter — Future Self

Write a detailed letter from your future self, 10 years from now, describing the life you have built.

Steps

  1. 1Set a timer for 30 minutes. Write as if you are your future self, 10 years from today.
  2. 2Describe your typical Tuesday: what time you wake up, what your work is, how your relationships feel, where you live, what your spiritual life looks like, how your family is thriving.
  3. 3Write what you are proud of. What struggles you overcame. What you gave up. What you protected.
  4. 4End with one line of advice to your present self — the most important thing to do or stop doing right now.

Reflection Prompts

"What future am I building by living today as I am living today?"
"Is the future I described actually possible — or do I need to believe bigger?"
🔧 Tool

The Life Vision Board — In Writing

A structured written vision across every major domain of your life — not vague wishes, but specific, vivid pictures.

How to Use It

Write 2–3 vivid sentences for each domain describing what "thriving" looks like in 5–10 years:

  1. FFaith: My spiritual life looks like… I am known for my faith in…
  2. FFamily: My marriage/family looks like… My children experience me as…
  3. HHealth: My body feels like… I am able to…
  4. VVocation: My work is… I am contributing to the world by…
  5. $Finances: My financial life gives me the freedom to… I have built…
  6. GGrowth: I am known for my expertise in… I am constantly learning…

Key Insight

🔑 A vision that doesn't include your family and faith is not a full vision — it's a career plan. Build a vision big enough to contain everything that matters.
🪞 Reflection

The Purpose Statement — Your Why

Distill your entire reason for being into one clear, memorable sentence that guides every major decision you make.

The Formula

Use this structure to draft your purpose statement:

My purpose is to [verb] [who] so that [impact/outcome] — through [your unique gifts/approach].

Example: "My purpose is to build and lead with integrity so that my family, my team, and my community experience what it means to live with faith and courage — through honest work, deep relationships, and continuous growth."

Refinement Questions

"Does this statement reflect my faith? Would I stand behind it even if it cost me something?"
"Is this purpose about me — or is it genuinely about something larger than me?"
🌱 Practice

The Eulogy Exercise — Legacy

Write the eulogy you want someone to give at your funeral. Then ask: am I living in a way that makes that eulogy true?

Steps

  1. 1Write 3 eulogies — one from your spouse/closest family member, one from a colleague or person you served, one from your children or a younger person you mentored.
  2. 2Read them aloud. How does it feel?
  3. 3Identify the gap between the person described and who you are today. That gap is your design brief.

Reflection Prompts

"What do I want to be remembered for — and does the way I spend my time reflect that?"
Phase 4
Week 7 – 8

The Blueprint
Goals That Are Aligned, Not Just Ambitious

"A goal without a plan is a wish. A plan without a vision is noise."

Most goal-setting fails because it's disconnected from deeper purpose. In this phase, you translate your vision into a structured 1-year plan — with seasonal goals, 90-day sprints, and clear priorities — so your effort compounds instead of scatters.

🔧 Tool

The 3-Level Goal Architecture

A cascading structure that links your 10-year vision → 1-year goals → 90-day sprints → weekly actions.

The Structure

  1. L1Annual Themes (3 max): One word or phrase per domain that captures what this year is about. E.g. "Foundation Year," "Health Rebuilt," "Business Launched."
  2. L2Quarterly Goals (3–5 per quarter): Specific, measurable outcomes you will achieve in 90 days. Each goal must tie back to your annual theme.
  3. L3Weekly Actions (3 per week): The 3 highest-value actions this week that move your quarterly goals forward.

Key Rule

🔑 Never have more than 3 active goals at one time. Focus is the multiplier. The person who chases 10 goals achieves none of them. Choose 3 that would change everything.
✏️ Exercise

The Filter Test — Does This Goal Belong?

Before committing to any goal, run it through 4 questions that reveal if it's truly yours or borrowed from someone else's expectations.

The 4 Questions

  1. 1Is it mine? Am I pursuing this because I genuinely want it — or because of what others will think?
  2. 2Is it aligned? Does achieving this move me closer to my vision, values, and purpose — or is it a detour?
  3. 3Is the cost worth it? What will I have to sacrifice to achieve this? Am I willing to pay that price?
  4. 4Does it honor my non-negotiables? Can I pursue this without sacrificing my faith, my family, or my health?

Reflection Prompts

"Am I pursuing this goal to build something — or to prove something?"
🔧 Tool

The Annual Blueprint — 1-Page Plan

Condense your entire year's design onto one page. This becomes your reference document — your "north star" for every major decision in the year.

The Blueprint Sections

  1. 1This Year's Theme: One word that captures what this year is about.
  2. 2My Purpose Statement: (from Phase 3)
  3. 3Top 3 Goals: The 3 outcomes that would make this year transformational.
  4. 4My Non-Negotiables: What I will protect no matter what.
  5. 5Stop Doing List: 3 things I am officially done with this year.
  6. 6My Word for the Year: One word I want to embody and grow into.

Key Insight

🔑 Print this. Put it where you will see it daily. Review it every Sunday. A plan you never see is a plan you never follow.
🪞 Reflection

The Subtraction Exercise — Stop Doing List

Identify the commitments, habits, relationships, and beliefs that are silently consuming your capacity for the life you want.

Steps

  1. 1List everything you currently spend time on (commitments, apps, meetings, habits, relationships).
  2. 2Mark each: Essential / Valuable / Draining / Irrelevant.
  3. 3Everything "Draining" or "Irrelevant" is a candidate for elimination.
  4. 4Choose 3 things to stop doing immediately. Write them as formal commitments.

Reflection Prompts

"What am I doing out of guilt, obligation, or habit — that no longer serves my design?"
"What would I have more of — time, energy, peace — if I stopped doing ______?"
Phase 5
Week 9 – 10

The Engine
Time, Habits, and Daily Discipline

"You don't rise to your goals. You fall to your systems."

Vision without a daily engine stays a dream. This phase builds the operational layer of your life: how you spend your days, what habits anchor your growth, and how you protect your time without burning out or abandoning what matters.

🔧 Tool

The Ideal Week Design

Design your ideal week from scratch — before life fills it for you. Block time for your most important roles, not just your most urgent tasks.

Steps

  1. 1Start with your non-negotiables: faith practice, family time, health. Block these first — they are immovable.
  2. 2Identify your 2–3 hours of peak energy each day. Protect this time exclusively for deep work on your top priority.
  3. 3Create 3 "zones": Deep Work (focus, creation), Shallow Work (admin, meetings), and Recovery (rest, family, faith).
  4. 4Leave 20% of your week unscheduled — for overflow, emergence, and rest.
  5. 5Map your actual week against your ideal. Notice the gaps and redesign.

Key Insight

🔑 "Someday" is not a day of the week. If faith, family, and health are not scheduled, they will be perpetually postponed by whatever feels urgent.
🌱 Practice

The Daily Anchor Routine — Morning & Evening

Design a simple, sustainable morning and evening routine that sets the tone for your day and closes it with intention.

Morning Anchor (30–60 min)

  1. 1Silence/Prayer (10 min): Before your phone. Center yourself in your faith and purpose.
  2. 2Review your Blueprint (3 min): Read your purpose, goals, and non-negotiables. Set your intention.
  3. 3Move your body (15 min): Whatever form — walk, stretch, gym. Non-negotiable.
  4. 4Identify your 3 priorities for the day (5 min): The 3 actions that, if done, make today a success.

Evening Anchor (15 min)

  1. 1Review your day (5 min): What did you accomplish? What was left undone?
  2. 2Gratitude (3 min): Write 3 specific things you're grateful for from today.
  3. 3Set tomorrow's 3 priorities (3 min): Close the loop so your mind can rest.
  4. 4Disconnect (by a set time): Choose a hard cutoff for screens. Protect sleep as a non-negotiable.

Key Insight

🔑 Don't build a 2-hour morning routine. Build a 30-minute one you actually keep. Consistency over complexity, every time.
✏️ Exercise

The Habit Stack — Build on What You Already Do

Attach new growth habits to existing behaviors so they require no extra willpower to execute.

The Formula

After I [EXISTING HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT] for [DURATION].

Steps

  1. 1List 5 things you do automatically every day without thinking (make coffee, brush teeth, sit at your desk, eat lunch).
  2. 2Identify 3 habits you want to build this quarter (e.g. read, pray, journal, exercise, call family).
  3. 3Pair each new habit to an existing anchor. Start with just 2–5 minutes per habit.
  4. 4Run the stack for 30 days before adding anything new.

Reflection Prompts

"What single habit, if practiced daily, would most compound into the person I want to become?"
🔧 Tool

The Anti-Burnout Protocol

Build recovery and renewal directly into your schedule so that high performance is sustainable — not a sprint toward collapse.

The 4 Recovery Layers

  1. DDaily: Build a hard stop time. After that time, no work-related thinking. Protect at least 1 hour of unstructured rest or family presence.
  2. WWeekly: One full day without work. Not a light day — a full Sabbath-like rest. Protect this as a spiritual and biological necessity.
  3. QQuarterly: One day each quarter for a personal retreat — to review, reflect, and recalibrate your plan. (See Phase 6.)
  4. AAnnually: At minimum one week of genuine vacation — no devices, no "just checking in." Real renewal requires real withdrawal.

Key Insight

🔑 Rest is not the absence of work. It is the preparation for better work. The person who rests well, works better. The person who never rests, eventually cannot work at all.
Phase 6
Ongoing — For Life

The Rhythm
Review, Recalibrate, Sustain

"The goal is not to finish the framework. It is to never need to start over."

Life does not stay still. Seasons change, priorities shift, children grow, opportunities emerge. This phase installs a rhythmic review system — weekly, monthly, quarterly — so your life design stays current, alive, and responsive without requiring a complete rebuild every year.

🌱 Practice

The Weekly Review — 30 Minutes That Changes Everything

A structured weekly review that keeps you on track, prevents drift, and ensures your days are aligned with your design.

The 5-Step Weekly Review (Sunday evening or Monday morning)

  1. 1Clear the deck (5 min): Process all notes, tasks, and commitments from the past week. Close open loops.
  2. 2Review your Blueprint (3 min): Re-read your purpose, goals, and non-negotiables.
  3. 3Score your week (5 min): Rate each life domain 1–5 for this week. Notice any that are consistently low.
  4. 4Identify next week's 3 priorities (5 min): The 3 actions that will most advance your top goals.
  5. 5Block time for priorities (5 min): Open your calendar and schedule the 3 priorities before anything else fills the week.

The One Question That Anchors It All

"If I kept living exactly as I lived this past week for the next 5 years — would I end up where I want to go?"
✏️ Exercise

The Quarterly Retreat — Your 4-Hour Recalibration

Once every 90 days, step completely away from your normal environment to review, reflect, and redesign for the next season.

The Retreat Agenda (4 hours)

  1. 1Arrival & Silence (20 min): No phone. Begin with prayer, gratitude, or stillness. Let your mind decompress before thinking.
  2. 2Last Quarter Review (45 min): What did I accomplish? What fell short? What surprised me? What do I want to stop, start, continue?
  3. 3Life Wheel Re-Score (20 min): Run your Phase 1 wheel again. What has improved? What has slipped?
  4. 4Blueprint Update (45 min): Revise your goals, priorities, and non-negotiables based on the new season.
  5. 5Next Quarter Plan (45 min): Set 3 goals for the next 90 days. Design your ideal week for the new season.
  6. 6Gratitude & Closure (15 min): Write what you're most grateful for this quarter. End in prayer or reflection.

Key Insight

🔑 You don't need a new vision every year. You need the courage to keep executing the one you already have — and the wisdom to adjust it as you grow.
🪞 Reflection

The Annual Life Review — Your Year in Full

A comprehensive end-of-year reflection that closes the chapter fully so you can begin the next one with clarity and intentionality.

Key Questions

  1. →What were the 3 best things that happened this year — and why?
  2. →What did I fail at — and what did that teach me?
  3. →What did I learn about myself that I didn't know a year ago?
  4. →Who did I become this year? Is that the person I set out to become?
  5. →How did I show up for my family? For my faith? For my health?
  6. →What do I want next year to be defined by?

Reflection Prompts

"Did I live this year on purpose — or did I just let it happen to me?"
"What story will I tell about this year, and is it a story I'm proud of?"
🌱 Practice

The Accountability Structure — Don't Go Alone

Build the human infrastructure that will hold you to your design when motivation fades and life pushes back.

The 3 Accountability Relationships

  1. 1The Mentor: Someone 10–20 years ahead of you who has built the kind of life you admire. Meet quarterly. Ask for their honest assessment of your trajectory.
  2. 2The Peer Partner: Someone at your level doing the same work of intentional life design. Share your Blueprint. Check in weekly. Challenge each other.
  3. 3Your Spouse/Family: If you're married, your spouse should know your Blueprint. They are not just your support system — they are a co-designer of your shared life. Review your family vision together at minimum once a year.

Key Insight

🔑 Accountability is not surveillance. It is the gift of someone who cares enough about your growth to tell you the truth when you're drifting.
